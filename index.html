VIEW FROM
SOMEWHERE

This exhibition examines how journalism organizations market themselves - not to analyze advertising creativity, but to understand how the profession explains its social function under shifting economic and technological conditions.

The exhibition treats self-promotional campaigns as primary source material. Slogans are compressed statements of institutional identity. They show what journalism promises when it must persuade rather than presume authority.

EVIDENCE // CAMPAIGN_CASE_STUDIES

CAMPAIGN
ARCHIVE

Across print, broadcast, cable, digital, and subscription-era campaigns, news organizations have used slogans, brand promises, and promotional language to define what journalism is supposed to do. These messages are not neutral.

MTV // 1981 BROADCAST ERA

I WANT MY MTV

MTV revolutionized media branding by selling an attitude, an aesthetic, and a lifestyle. The broadcast boom introduced entirely new methodologies for claiming authority. "I Want My MTV!" used celebrities to pressure cable operators to carry the channel, turning brand promotion into a public mobilization campaign. MTV pioneered techniques journalism would later adopt: selling identity, attitude, and cultural allegiance rather than simply content.

BROADCAST ERA // LIFESTYLE_BRANDING [ MTV ]
REUTERS // 2024 AI ERA

PURE NEWS

As Generative AI began to flood the internet with frictionless, synthetic text and hyper-realistic fake imagery, Reuters pivoted its marketing strategy to focus entirely on human verification. The campaign dramatically visualized the threat of misinformation, using imagery of water turning murky to represent the pollution of digital truth. This marks a critical evolution in media self-promotion: the absence of AI intervention and human editorial oversight has become a premium commodity.

AI ERA // HUMAN_VERIFICATION [ REUTERS ]
THE GUARDIAN // 1986 ENDURING BENCHMARK

POINTS OF VIEW

Considered an absolute classic and enduring benchmark for media advertising. The Guardian's 1986 campaign brilliantly illustrated the necessity of full journalistic context. The iconic television spot depicted a dramatic street scene from three distinct camera angles: one suggesting a skinhead was attacking a businessman, another showing him fleeing the police, and finally, the "whole picture" revealing he was actually saving the man from falling bricks. Facts without context can lie.

CNN deployed one of its most brutally simple campaigns in response to an era defined by "alternative facts" and political polarization. A stark, static shot of an apple. A calm voiceover. The campaign stripped away the bravado of cable news to focus on the absolute baseline of journalistic integrity: objective reality.

COUNTERSTRIKE OF THE FREE PRESS

Finland's Helsingin Sanomat bypassed Russian state censorship using a highly unconventional medium: the video game Counter-Strike. The newspaper created a custom map within the globally popular game, secretly embedding independent, factual reporting about the war in Ukraine in a virtual environment. The campaign generated nearly 4 billion PR impressions and successfully delivered critical, uncensored news to young men - a demographic notoriously difficult to reach through traditional journalistic channels.

UNSUBSCRIBE

In a brilliant subversion of the standard marketing pitch, The Philadelphia Inquirer challenged readers to "Unsubscribe from stereotypes" and "Unsubscribe from corruption" - and instead, "Subscribe to The Inquirer." This provocative initiative flipped the narrative of civic fatigue. By leveraging profound local patriotism and inherent "Philly bias," the campaign redefined purchasing a regional subscription as a definitive act of civic duty and community engagement.

HELSINGIN SANOMAT // 2019

THE CLIMATE FONT

A free typeface dynamically designed to "melt" and lose its weight over a simulated timeline, visually representing the devastating impact of rising global temperatures. Environmental data turned into a tangible, usable design asset. The font weights represent years from 1979 to 2050. Typography turned into activism.

CLIMATE // DESIGN_AS_JOURNALISM
TIMES OF INDIA // 2019

UNPLASTIC INDIA

A visually arresting campaign leveraging the physical medium of the newspaper itself. Detailed illustrations of marine animals choking on plastic waste, printed directly within the daily broadsheet. By using the tactile nature of print to force a visceral, emotional confrontation with reality, the newspaper shocked its readership into awareness. INMA Silver, Spikes Asia Silver.

ENVIRONMENTAL // PRINT_AS_ACTIVISM
eNCA // SOUTH AFRICA

QUESTION. THINK. ACT.

A concise command rather than a comforting slogan, eNCA's campaign presents journalism as civic activation rather than passive consumption. Launched during a period of intense political and social tension in South Africa, it positioned the news channel as a catalyst for engaged citizenship. News becomes a starting point for participation, not an endpoint for information.

CIVIC_ACTIVATION // PUBLIC_SERVICE
ABC NEWS AUSTRALIA // 2019

YOURS

ABC's "Yours" compresses the entire public-service broadcasting ethos into a single possessive word. Instead of stressing competitive superiority or technical reach, it suggests belonging, shared ownership, and institutional accountability. "Yours" asserts that ABC News Australia is answerable to its audience in a fundamental, structural way. Public-service journalism belongs to everyone.

THEORY
ARCHIVE

Seen together across decades and markets, slogans map a profession continually redefining its value proposition in response to crisis. These essays form the theoretical backbone of the exhibition.

01
ESSAY HISTORICAL OVERVIEW

How Journalism Learned to Advertise Itself

News organizations have always sold more than news. In the print era, trust was often implied rather than argued. Newspapers leaned on locality, routine, and civic belonging: your city, your street, your nation, your daily habit. Broadcast changed the tone. Radio and television introduced voices, faces, live transmission, and the performance of institutional authority. The cable era intensified competition - slogans such as "Fair and Balanced" and "We Report. You Decide." turned objectivity itself into a promotional weapon. In the digital era, campaigns like "The Truth Is Hard" and "Democracy Dies in Darkness" reframed journalism as a difficult, embattled, and socially necessary practice. Journalism no longer just claimed credibility; it fought for it in a crowded and partisan market.

"ALL THE NEWS THAT'S FIT TO PRINT," "FAIR AND BALANCED," "THE TRUTH IS HARD," "SUSCRIBETE A LOS HECHOS"
02
ESSAY INSTITUTIONAL ANALYSIS

Public Service vs Commercial Rhetoric

Not all journalism speaks about itself in the same way. Public-service media and commercial media often use similar values - trust, accuracy, relevance, independence - but they frame those values differently because they answer to different institutional logics. Public-service rhetoric usually emphasizes mandate. It speaks in the language of service, universality, belonging, and public obligation. ABC News Australia's "Yours" is a good example: a very small word that implies ownership by the public, not by the market. Commercial rhetoric more often emphasizes differentiation. It needs to persuade audiences to choose, subscribe, return, and pay. The tension matters because it shapes how journalism justifies itself. One stresses duty, the other persuasion. Both seek trust, but they speak to the public in different accents.

"YOURS," "TRUST IS EARNED," "THE TRUTH IS WORTH IT," "MOST WATCHED. MOST TRUSTED."
03
ESSAY GEOGRAPHY OF MEDIA

Local, National, Global

Journalism does not only tell people what happened. It also tells them where they belong. Across decades of self-promotion, media organizations have repeatedly defined themselves through scale: local, national, or global. Local rhetoric is intimate. It promises proximity, familiarity, and shared daily life. "It's where you live." "Your Region. Your Paper." National rhetoric is broader and often more political - it links journalism to citizenship, nation-building, or democratic renewal. "Building the Nation." "Your right to know. A new voice for a new Pakistan." Global rhetoric promises reach, perspective, and connection across borders. Al Jazeera and Deutsche Welle have branded themselves through humanity, intellect, and worldliness. Placed side by side, these slogans reveal that journalism is always selling geography as well as information. Is this your paper, your country, or your world?

"IT'S WHERE YOU LIVE," "BUILDING THE NATION," "HEAR THE HUMAN STORY"
04
ESSAY AI ERA

Trust Labels

For much of modern media history, trust was presumed. Today it is increasingly labeled, audited, and displayed. In polluted digital environments, journalism has begun to market not only content, but visible signs of credibility. This shift reflects a deeper change. When authority is unstable, institutions must show their workings. Editorial guidelines, verification practices, transparency statements, and third-party trust initiatives all turn internal process into public-facing evidence. The claim is no longer simply "believe us," but "see how this was made." Industry-wide trust campaigns make this especially clear. The Pennsylvania NewsMedia Association's "Trust in Newspapers" campaign treats trust as a collective asset that can be measured, packaged, and promoted. Trust labels and certification schemes function like seals of quality in a marketplace crowded with misinformation, synthetic media, and blurred boundaries between editorial, sponsored, and manipulated content. Credibility becomes graphic: a badge, a label, a standard, a mark.

"FACTS FIRST," "TRUST IN NEWSPAPERS," "PURE NEWS, STRAIGHT FROM THE SOURCE"
05
ESSAY OPEN QUESTION

What Will Journalism Promise Next?

Every era produces its own journalistic promise. In one period the promise was completeness; in another, neutrality; in another, speed. More recently, journalism has promised courage, democracy, context, community, and independence from platform power or owner influence. These promises are never neutral. They appear most forcefully when journalism feels threatened - by war, polarization, distrust, collapsing business models, or technological upheaval. A slogan is never just a slogan; it is a compressed response to crisis. In an environment saturated with AI-generated text, synthetic imagery, and platform-mediated attention, news organizations may need to promise provenance, transparency, traceability, and human editorial accountability with a new intensity. So what will journalism promise next? Better facts, clearer methods, human verification, public usefulness, civic courage - or simply a reason to keep believing that shared reality can still be built in public.

"That is why the future question is not simply what journalism will do, but how it will explain itself."

- VIEW FROM SOMEWHERE

ABOUT
THE AUTHOR

Jakub Górnicki
Journalist // Exhibition Designer // Warsaw

Jakub Górnicki operates at the intersection of reality and art. As a journalist, exhibition designer, and creator of Outriders and Reakcja, he pioneers "Artistic Journalism" - a movement built as a visceral antidote to digital fatigue. His pioneering approach to immersive storytelling has been recognized with prestigious accolades, including the European Press Prize and INMA awards.

His work transforms rigorous reportage into physical, live experiences, stripping away traditional media norms to push audiences into questioning what they know and what they believe. Blending curatorial rigor with journalistic practice, the exhibition does not celebrate advertising creativity. Instead, it critically dissects how news organizations attempt to justify their value when their authority can no longer be blindly assumed.

Based in Poland and working internationally, Górnicki has presented his projects across Europe and North America, consistently creating spaces that make editorial thinking not just visible, but deeply experiential.
